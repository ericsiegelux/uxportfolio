Inspiration

Click to see my project!

I was taking an information studies course over the 2019 summer break and we had to make a hypertext link portrait, which was something that was created through HTML and CSS uniquely representing us. As someone who loves music, I decided to create a face composed of my favorite albums of all time (starting from the top).

I enjoyed making this "portrait," but it was incredibly tedious to do, album by album. I knew there must be a better way to directly extract album covers from popular streaming services and make different mosaics out of them.

That leads us to the problem: How can we offer users the ability to create mosaics with ease?

The Pitch

Screenshot from my pitch

I contacted my good friend Ram with a more fleshed-out version of my initial idea. Ram is a computer science major at UCLA who I knew would be able to help me bring my idea to life. As seen above, I sent him a custom "pitch" of my idea to create a website/application (at the time) that would connect the user's streaming service, ask the user for a photo, select the tiles of the mosaic from a selection menu, and return the resulting mosaic picture.

Ram responded "Dang this is sick haha...Love this."

I successfully recruited my backend developer for this project.

Who are our users?

Focus #1:
Music Streaming Service Preference

To start developing Musaic, Ram and I had to decide which music platform to focus on. Since we were only a two-person team, Ram said he would only be able to develop Musaic with one streaming service's API to roll out Musaic in a reasonable time. I was biased because I have Apple Music, but I knew we needed to collect data on people's choices in streaming services. Here are results from a survey I sent out regarding streaming services:

I deployed the survey in two places: on UCLA's student page and on a subReddit called Sample Size where anyone can fill out a survey. Ram and I knew we would want our friends at school to use Musaic, but we were also curious to see what streaming service is popular in the general public.


Winner: Spotify

Focus #2:
Spotify Playlist Usage

I wanted to know how popular sharing personal Spotify playlists were among our survey respondents:

There is a roughly 50/50 split for people who have personal Spotify playlists to share them amongst friends. This was a good sign for Musaic because we would be able to offer a new and exciting way for Spotify users who don't share their playlists, for whatever reason, to share them.

Initial User Flow

For creating the Musaic, the user would go through three steps:

The reasoning behind having the order of these three steps was to have the users realize right away that they needed a Spotify account instead of learning they needed a Spotify account after uploading a personal photo. The upload of the personal photo was chosen to be before selecting a playlist because visually, we want users to create a visual hierarchy of laying the "foundation" of their Musaic (the personal photo) first and adding the tiles (the album covers) afterward.

Initial Wireframe

After creating the Initial user flow, I had to visualize what this user flow would look like in webpages. I wanted to create rectangular buttons to, in some way, resemble the tiles of Musaics, despite betraying most modern UI trends.

Below is what the inital HTML pages looked like. There are a few changes from the grayscale wireframe to the HTML pages:

The vertical positioning of the visualizations in the wireframe to the horizontal positioning in the HTML pages. This change was made to create more space below for other items more visible such as provided photos (more about that below) and playlists.

The playlist selection page in the HTML pages. Ram and I agreed that not only is it easier to program the other confirmation page, but having one page dedicated to confirming the playlist would require the focus of the user more than an overlay on the same page, therefore reducing mistakes in generating Musaics.

Style Guide

With the HTML pages set in place, we needed to add some CSS files, which required a style guide.

Besides black and white, I was looking for colors that would contrast well with the Spotify logo and would allow muasics, photos, and playlists to stand out well against the backdrop.

Logo Evolution

I wanted to take on the task of creating Musaic's logo. Above are some initial ideas I had for what concepts of music and mosaics I could integrate into our logo.

The process of creating Musaic's logos involved a lot of trial and error. I knew I wanted to integrate a grid into the logo and initially, I thought of making a musical note out of a colored grid. After more discussion with Ram, we thought adding a smoother music note would look cleaner.

I was also deadset on having a gradient, like Instagram or Apple Music. One of my mentors told me gradients are fun, but they are hard to look at in logos. That is how Musaic's logo landed on the charcoal gray background with a black and white checkered pattern note.

While the Musaic note logo was a big accomplishment for me, Musaic needed another logo that involved the name. I tried to integrate the Musaic note logo into the word 'Musaic,' but it was hard to see the detailed note logo. This led to the simple blue note replacing the 'i' in Musaic.

Improvements

Most of these improvements are a result of Musaic being comprised of a two-person team but still should be highlighted:

  • Size of the Musaic File and Detail of the Musaic File

There is an inherent tradeoff with having a detailed picture of little albums and the size of the picture. We couldn't make the file too big because of the generation time and load time. The tradeoff with that is not being able to see the detailed albums when zooming all the way in, especially on mobile.

  • Design Choices vs Reality

Working on Musaic taught me the importance of knowing the limitations of front end development and back end development. Not all of my initial UI designs could be implemented because of the small team and small scale of the project.

  • Using Different Streaming Services

Other streaming services like Apple Music or Amazon Music have APIs for us to use, but as mentioned earlier, we choose to focus on one platform.

  • Social Media Sharing

I initially wanted the capability of directly sharing Musaics from our site. Again, this was a challenge for Ram to invest his valuable time in.
